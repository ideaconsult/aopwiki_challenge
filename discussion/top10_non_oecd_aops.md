# Top 10 Most Promising Non-OECD Endorsed AOPs
*Analysis conducted via AOP-Wiki MCP Tool | May 2026*

---

## Methodology & Selection Criteria

AOPs were retrieved across multiple biological domains using keyword searches (oxidative stress, DNA damage, mitochondrial dysfunction, ionizing radiation). From the pool of results, all AOPs with OECD status of **"WPHA/WNT Endorsed"** were excluded. AOPs still formally **"Under Development"** or **"Under Review"** were also excluded, leaving only those with a blank OECD status — i.e., not yet entered the OECD endorsement pipeline.

Candidates were then ranked on four criteria:

| Criterion | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Structural completeness** | Number of Key Events (KEs) and Key Event Relationships (KERs) defined — more links = stronger mechanistic narrative |
| **Assay coverage** | Fraction of KEs with validated measurement methods — enables experimental verification |
| **Adverse outcome relevance** | Human health or ecotoxicological significance of the AO endpoint |
| **Stressor diversity** | Number of named stressors mapped — indicates real-world applicability |

### 1. AOP200 — Estrogen Receptor Activation Leading to Breast Cancer
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Agonism, Estrogen Receptor (KE111)
**Adverse Outcome:** Breast Cancer (KE1193)
**Key Events:** 20 | **KERs:** 24 | **Stressors:** 0 (EDC class-level)

**Why it is promising:**
This is the most structurally elaborate AOP in the dataset with 20 KEs and 24 KERs, spanning molecular, cellular and tissue levels. The pathway is biologically grounded in decades of estrogen receptor pharmacology, covers both genomic (classical ER→DNA binding) and non-genomic (second messenger, kinase) branches, and incorporates multiple measurable KEs including cell proliferation, DNA damage, oxidative stress, migration, invasion and angiogenesis — nearly all with validated assays. Breast cancer accounts for the highest incidence of any cancer in women globally, making this AOP highly policy-relevant. The existence of well-established in vitro ER transactivation assays (BG1Luc, CALUX, YES) at the MIE makes it immediately testable. The primary gap is lack of formal KER weight-of-evidence narratives, but the mechanistic density is exceptional — this AOP is a strong candidate for expedited OECD review.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- ER Agonism (6 HTS assay formats)
- Oxidative Stress (19 assay methods)
- DNA Damage (10 methods including OECD TG 473/487)
- Cell Proliferation (Ki-67 IHC, BrdU)

### 2. AOP409 — Frustrated Phagocytosis Leads to Malignant Mesothelioma
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Frustrated Phagocytosis (KE1668)
**Adverse Outcome:** Increased Mesotheliomas (KE1090)
**Key Events:** 6 | **KERs:** 7 | **Stressors:** 6 (asbestos, carbon nanotubes, mineral fibres, etc.)

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP is notable for its direct regulatory relevance to fibrous particle risk assessment — covering asbestos, carbon nanotubes, and other high-aspect-ratio materials (HARN). The mechanistic chain from frustrated phagocytosis → ROS → secondary genotoxicity → genomic instability → proliferation → mesothelioma is linear, coherent, and supported by a substantial body of literature. With 6 named stressors, it has the broadest chemical/material applicability in this shortlist. All upstream KEs have multiple measurement assays (microscopy for phagocytosis, ELISA/PCR for cytokines, comet assay/γH2AX for DNA damage, BrdU/Ki-67 for proliferation). Mesothelioma is a rare, highly fatal cancer with no approved curative treatment; regulatory tools to predict fibre carcinogenicity have high public health value. This AOP fills an important gap in particle toxicology not yet addressed by OECD test guidelines.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Frustrated Phagocytosis (SEM, light microscopy, time-lapse)
- Pro-inflammatory mediators (ELISA, cytokine arrays, IHC)
- DNA Damage & Mutation (comet, micronucleus, γH2AX, Ames)
- Cell Proliferation (BrdU, Ki-67, flow cytometry)

### 3. AOP451 — Interaction with Lung Resident Cell Membrane Components Leads to Lung Cancer
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Substance interaction with lung cell membrane components (KE1495)
**Adverse Outcome:** Lung Cancer (KE1670)
**Key Events:** 7 | **KERs:** 8 | **Stressors:** 6 (diesel exhaust particles, nanosized particles, carbon black, etc.)

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP provides an inhalation-specific mechanistic framework for particle-induced lung cancer — the leading cause of cancer death worldwide. It follows a clear inflammatory carcinogenesis sequence: membrane interaction → cytokine secretion → inflammatory cell recruitment → cytotoxicity → ROS → secondary genotoxicity → DNA damage/mutation → proliferation → lung cancer. It is one of the few AOPs to explicitly invoke the concept of *secondary genotoxicity* (ROS-mediated, not direct chemical binding), which is mechanistically important for regulatory classification of particles under the GHS. Six named stressors (including diesel exhaust PM, carbon black, and nanoparticles) make this immediately useful for occupational and environmental risk assessment. The KERs are formally documented, and most upstream KEs carry assay methods.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Membrane interaction (ELISA, Western blot, PCLS)
- Pro-inflammatory mediators (ELISA, PCR arrays, IHC)
- ROS (H2-DCF-DA, lipid peroxidation, NBT)
- DNA Damage & Mutation (comet, micronucleus, γH2AX, Ames)
- Cell Proliferation (BrdU, Ki-67, CyQuant)

### 4. AOP432 — Deposition of Energy by Ionizing Radiation Leading to Acute Myeloid Leukemia
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Deposition of Energy (KE1686)
**Adverse Outcome:** Acute Myeloid Leukemia (KE1955)
**Key Events:** 7 | **KERs:** 0 (KERs not yet defined) | **Stressors:** 1 (ionizing radiation)

**Why it is promising:**
Despite having no formal KERs documented yet, this AOP addresses an extremely high-priority endpoint: radiation-induced AML, which is one of the best-characterised radiation-associated malignancies in atomic bomb survivors and radiotherapy patients. The KE chain (energy deposition → RONS → DNA damage → inadequate repair → mutations → proliferation → immune suppression → AML) follows a plausible and well-supported biological logic. All molecular and cellular KEs have robust assay methods, including dosimetry tools (FNTD, TEPC, Monte Carlo simulations) at the MIE. The primary developmental need is formalisation of KERs with weight-of-evidence assessments, making this a high-priority target for structured development. Its regulatory value for radiation protection standards (ICRP, UNSCEAR) is considerable.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Energy Deposition (Monte Carlo, FNTD, TEPC, dosimeters)
- RONS (Amplex Red, DHE, MitoSox, TBARS, nitroguanine)
- DNA Damage (comet, γH2AX, PFGE, OECD TGs)
- Inadequate DNA Repair (γH2AX foci, 53BP1, alkaline elution)
- Mutations in Critical Genes (Ames, Big Blue)

### 5. AOP396 — Deposition of Ionizing Energy Leads to Population Decline via Impaired Meiosis
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Deposition of Ionizing Energy (KE1550)
**Adverse Outcomes:** Decreased egg production/fecundity; Decreased reproduction; Decreased population size
**Key Events:** 9 | **KERs:** 16 | **Stressors:** 2

**Why it is promising:**
This is the most structurally complete ionizing radiation AOP in the non-endorsed pool, with 9 KEs, 16 KERs, and — unusually — three adverse outcome endpoints spanning individual and population levels. It addresses reproductive toxicity in a model organism (*C. elegans*) with direct relevance to ecotoxicological risk assessment for nuclear/radiological contamination. The dual pathway (ROS → oxidative stress → oocyte apoptosis; AND DNA damage → meiotic chromosome disruption → spermatogenesis reduction) is mechanistically sophisticated and supported by literature in multiple species. The population-level adverse outcome (KE997: Decreased population size) makes this particularly valuable for ecological risk assessment frameworks. KERs are well-defined with upstream/downstream connectivity documented. Multiple KEs have established assays, though organ- and population-level endpoints lack standardised methods — a tractable gap.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- ROS (H2-DCF-DA, lipid peroxidation, NBT)
- Oxidative Stress (19 methods including Seahorse XFp)
- DNA Damage (comet, PFGE, HPLC, γH2AX)
- Meiotic chromosome dynamics (FISH, time-lapse GFP microscopy)
- Cell cycle disruption (flow cytometry, FACS, [3H]thymidine)

### 6. AOP443 — DNA Damage and Mutations Leading to Metastatic Breast Cancer
**OECD Status:** Under Development
**MIE:** Increased DNA Damage and Mutation (KE1669)
**Adverse Outcome:** Metastatic Breast Cancer (KE1982)
**Key Events:** 8 | **KERs:** 8 | **Stressors:** 1 (alcohol/acetaldehyde)

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP is notable for its mechanistic novelty — specifically the inclusion of epigenetic regulation via the miRNA–SIRT1–NF-κB axis as intermediate KEs linking DNA damage to metastatic progression. This is a rarely formalised pathway in AOP-Wiki and addresses the molecular basis of alcohol-associated breast cancer metastasis. The AO endpoint (metastatic breast cancer, KE1982) is one of the few AOPs in the database with validated assays at the adverse outcome level itself, including bioluminescence imaging, invasion/migration assays and immunohistochemistry. The EMT (Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition) KE is measurable by multiple methods. All 8 KERs are formally defined. The pathway is directly relevant to alcohol regulation and cancer prevention policy.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- DNA Damage & Mutation (comet, micronucleus, γH2AX, Ames)
- Inadequate DNA Repair (FM-HCR, γH2AX foci, 53BP1)
- Mutations (Ames, HPRT, TK, PIG-A, transgenic rodent assay)
- miRNA expression (qRT-PCR, microarray, RNA-seq)
- SIRT1 expression (qRT-PCR, Western blot, ELISA)
- NF-κB activation (reporter assay, Western blot, ELISA)
- EMT (Western blot, IHC, qRT-PCR, SNP array)
- Metastatic Breast Cancer (BLI, invasion/migration, IHC)

### 7. AOP497 — ERα Inactivation Leads to Insulin Resistance and Metabolic Syndrome
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Estrogen Receptor Alpha Inactivation (KE2126)
**Adverse Outcomes:** Increased Insulin Resistance (KE2119); Metabolic Syndrome (KE2145)
**Key Events:** 5 | **KERs:** 9 | **Stressors:** 0

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP addresses a highly topical endpoint — metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance — from an endocrine-disruption angle centred on ERα inactivation. It is one of the very few AOPs in the database linking sex hormone receptor biology explicitly to metabolic disease, filling a critical gap between endocrinology and cardiometabolic toxicology. The dual adverse outcomes (insulin resistance at the individual level; metabolic syndrome at the population level) give it breadth for both clinical and public health risk assessment. The KE chain is well-connected with 9 KERs for only 5 KEs, indicating strong mechanistic integration. Assays for mitochondrial dysfunction (Seahorse, membrane potential, ETS activity) and oxidative stress (19 methods) are mature. The insulin resistance AO has established clinical endpoints (HEC, HOMA-IR). Weakness: the MIE KE2126 currently lacks assays — this is a tractable gap for assay development.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- ROS (H2-DCF-DA, lipid peroxidation, NBT)
- Mitochondrial Dysfunction (Seahorse, ΔΨm, ATP, ETS)
- Oxidative Stress (19 methods)
- Insulin Resistance (HEC clamp, HOMA-IR)

### 8. AOP509 — Nrf2 Inhibition Leading to Vascular Disrupting Effects
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** NFE2/Nrf2 Repression (KE1417)
**Adverse Outcome:** Vascular Disrupting Effects (KE2161)
**Key Events:** 5 | **KERs:** 8 | **Stressors:** 0

**Why it is promising:**
The Nrf2 pathway is the master regulator of cellular antioxidant defence, and its inhibition is mechanistically linked to cardiovascular disease — the number one cause of mortality globally. This AOP is one of the few in AOP-Wiki that explicitly targets vascular toxicology, a domain under-represented in OECD test guidelines. The mechanistic chain (Nrf2 repression → oxidative stress → apoptosis + mitochondrial dysfunction → endothelial dysfunction → angiogenesis disruption → vascular disrupting effects) is biologically coherent and supported by the Nrf2 pharmacology literature. Eight KERs for 5 KEs indicates a well-connected graph. Oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction KEs carry extensive assay coverage. The AOP has direct relevance to the assessment of environmental pollutants, nanomaterials, and pharmaceuticals with known Nrf2-inhibitory activity. Gap: the MIE (Nrf2 repression) and downstream vascular KEs currently lack assay methods.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Oxidative Stress (19 methods including Seahorse, GSH, TBARS)
- Mitochondrial Dysfunction (ΔΨm, ETS, ATP, oxygen consumption)
- Apoptosis (TUNEL, Annexin V, caspase, Western blot)

### 9. AOP441 — Ionizing Radiation-Induced DNA Damage Leads to Microcephaly
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Deposition of Energy (KE1686)
**Adverse Outcome:** Increased Risk of Microcephaly (KE1978)
**Key Events:** 5 | **KERs:** 7 | **Stressors:** 1 (ionizing radiation)

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP addresses a unique developmental neurotoxicity endpoint — radiation-induced microcephaly — with direct public health relevance to prenatal exposures (medical imaging, radiotherapy, occupational and accidental exposures). The mechanistic centrepiece is p53 activation (KE1974) acting as a bifurcation node, driving both apoptosis and premature neuronal differentiation — a sophisticated and well-supported mechanism in developmental neurobiology. The resulting depletion of the neural progenitor pool mechanistically explains the macroscopic outcome of reduced brain growth. All 7 KERs are formally documented. The p53 activation and apoptosis KEs carry validated assays. This AOP is relevant to the developing field of developmental AOPs and connects to radiation protection guidelines for pregnant workers and patients. Its mechanistic clarity makes it a strong candidate for further development.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Energy Deposition (Monte Carlo, FNTD, TEPC, dosimeters)
- DNA Damage (comet, PFGE, HPLC, phospho-H2AX, OECD TGs)
- TP53 Activation (Western blot, immunocytochemistry)
- Apoptosis (TUNEL, Annexin V, comet, caspase, ELISA)

### 10. AOP335 — Chemical Cytotoxicity via Mitochondrial Impairment Leading to Urothelial Carcinogenesis
**OECD Status:** None (blank)
**MIE:** Mitochondrial Dysfunction (KE177)
**Adverse Outcome:** Urothelial Tumor (KE1763)
**Key Events:** 3 | **KERs:** 4 | **Stressors:** 3 (diuron, saccharin, cyanuric acid)

**Why it is promising:**
This AOP proposes a non-genotoxic carcinogenesis mode-of-action specifically for urothelial (bladder) cancer — a domain where regulatory science is actively debated. The cytotoxicity → regenerative hyperplasia → neoplasia sequence has been well-validated in rodent bioassays for several chemicals, and formalising it as an AOP framework enables more efficient read-across and classification. Three structurally diverse named stressors (herbicide diuron, artificial sweetener saccharin, and cyanuric acid from triazine disinfectants) demonstrate broad applicability across industrial chemicals and food additives. While concise (3 KEs, 4 KERs), the pathway is complete from MIE to AO with no gaps in the chain. The MIE — mitochondrial dysfunction — is one of the best-instrumented KEs in AOP-Wiki, with 7 validated assay methods. This AOP supports the development of non-animal testing strategies for the non-genotoxic carcinogen category. Its simplicity is a strength for regulatory adoption.

**Key measurable KEs with assays:**
- Mitochondrial Dysfunction (Seahorse O2 consumption, ΔΨm, ETS, ATP, PTP, mtDNA damage, ROS)
- Regenerative Cell Proliferation (assays not yet formalised — a development gap)
- Urothelial Hyperplasia (histopathology)

## Key Observations

**Cross-cutting themes:** Oxidative stress/ROS and DNA damage are shared KEs across the majority of selected AOPs, reflecting their central role in chemical- and radiation-induced toxicity. This network connectivity suggests opportunities for integrated testing strategies that cover multiple AOPs simultaneously.

**Gaps needing attention:** Several high-ranking AOPs (AOP432, AOP497, AOP509) have MIEs or late-stage KEs without validated assay methods. Filling these gaps through assay development should be prioritised to advance these pathways toward OECD review.

**Policy relevance:** The selected AOPs span cancer (breast, lung, bladder, mesothelioma, AML), developmental toxicity (microcephaly), reproductive toxicity (fertility, population decline), and metabolic disease (insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome) — covering the most significant areas of current regulatory concern.
